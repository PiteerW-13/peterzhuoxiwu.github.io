Act 1.3 - The Atmosphere, really "Atmospheric"!

Part I

"Let's go!"

As I gave the order, the spacecraft seemed to follow my order, automatically locked the destination, and immediately charged. With a "whoosh", like a bullet just out of the gun, it moved forward toward the locked target. After a while, we were already 10km above the surface.

"At this stop, we mainly study the structural characteristics and functions of the atmosphere. I will explain it in two parts, please listen carefully!"

"Okay, I'm ready, let's get started!" I replied impatiently.

As usual, PETE showed me a picture and asked: "Sir, where do you think the layers of clouds in this picture are located in the atmosphere?"

"That's babywork! Isn't it the first layer of the atmosphere - the troposphere? Our teacher has taught us about it!"

"Yes, sir, you are right this time. Let me start with a brief explanation: We all know that according to the temperature changes at different altitudes, the atmosphere can be divided into five layers - the troposphere, stratosphere, mesosphere, thermosphere (ionosphere), and exosphere."

  • Troposphere

    • It is the layer of the atmosphere closest to the earth's surface and the lowest layer of the atmosphere. The troposphere is affected by different terrains (such as forests, lakes, mountains, etc.), and the temperature changes are caused by sunlight, which causes vertical and horizontal winds, that is, a large amount of air convection occurs, so it is called the troposphere. Its height varies with latitude. The average height in low-latitude areas is 17~18 kilometers, the average height in mid-latitude areas is 10~12 kilometers, and the average height in the polar regions is 8~9 kilometers. Since this layer is the lowest layer of the atmosphere, it has the highest density and contains almost 75% of the total atmospheric mass. Therefore, the air can flow up and down in the troposphere, and weather phenomena such as wind, rain, thunder, and lightning occur in this layer.

  • Stratosphere

    • The stratosphere includes the stratosphere, inversion layer, and ozone layer. The layer from the top of the troposphere to an altitude of 50~55 KM is called the stratosphere. From the top of the troposphere to 35~40 The layer around KM, where the temperature hardly changes with altitude, is the stratosphere. From this layer to the top of the stratosphere, the temperature increases with altitude, which is the inversion layer. The stratosphere concentrates most of the ozone (O3) in the atmosphere and reaches its maximum value at an altitude of 20-25 KM, which is the ozone layer. The stratosphere contains ozone, which can absorb ultraviolet rays to protect the survival of all organisms on the earth and the surface from the deadly invasion of strong ultraviolet rays in the sun. Because the stratosphere is hot in the upper part and cold in the lower part, the advection movement is significant, and the atmosphere is stable without clouds and rain, it is suitable for aviation.

  • Mesosphere

    • It refers to the atmospheric layer from the top of the stratosphere to 85 kilometers. Since a low amount of ozone content in this layer, it cannot absorb a large number of solar ultraviolet rays, and most of the short-wave radiation that nitrogen and oxygen can absorb is absorbed by the upper atmosphere, so the temperature decreases with increasing altitude. Because the upper part of this layer of atmosphere is cold and the the lower part is warm, the air produces convection movement. However, due to the thin air in this layer, its convection movement cannot be compared with that of the troposphere. There are also many interesting phenomena in the mesosphere: noctilucent clouds appear at high latitudes, in summer, and at dusk, and abnormal weather in summer and winter, etc.

  • Thermosphere

    • It is located above the mesosphere and below the exosphere, and its top is about 800km from the ground. since the air in the thermosphere is highly ionized by the short-wave radiation from the sun, the ionosphere exists in this layer. In the thermosphere, the temperature rises rapidly with increasing altitude. This is because the ultraviolet radiation from the sun is absorbed by the atmospheric substances in this layer. The degree of warming is related to solar activity. When solar activity intensifies, the temperature rises rapidly with increasing altitude; conversely, when solar activity weakens, the temperature rises slowly with increasing altitude. Among them, there is also the aurora phenomenon that we are familiar with - electrons accelerated by the magnetic field in high latitudes will flow in, collide with atmospheric molecules in the thermosphere, and then be ionized. When those molecules return to their original state, they will produce a luminous phenomenon, which is called aurora.

  • Exosphere

    • It is the atmospheric layer above the thermosphere and the outermost layer of the Earth's atmosphere. The lower boundary of the exosphere is more than 800km from the surface, and the top boundary is about 2000-3000km in height. The temperature of the exosphere is extremely high, and the temperature is vertically distributed from low to high, and it increases with increasing altitude. Here, atmospheric molecules are heated because they absorb short-wave radiation from the sun. But because the atmospheric density of the exosphere is too low, it will not make people feel any warmth. Similar to the thermosphere, most of the molecules in this layer of air are ionized at the same time under the action of solar ultraviolet rays and cosmic rays. The air in the exosphere is extremely thin, and its density is almost the same as that of space, so it is also called the outer atmosphere.

    "This is the structure and characteristics of the five layers of the atmosphere, sir. Do you have any questions?"

    "I don't understand anything, but I remember that the teacher talked about the upper atmosphere in class. Why didn't you mention it when you introduced the five layers of the atmosphere just now?"

    "Sir, it seems that you have overlooked the premise I mentioned just now - temperature changes at different altitudes. This is only one of the conditions for analyzing the layers of the atmosphere. Of course, there are two other conditions:

    ① The distribution characteristics of atmospheric components with altitude

    It is divided into a uniform layer and a non-uniform layer.

  • The uniform layer

    • This refers to the space from the ground to about 80 kilometers. Due to the mixing effects of convection, turbulence, etc. caused by the uneven distribution of atmospheric temperature, air pressure, and number density, the volume percentage of each component of the atmosphere remains unchanged

  • The non-uniform layer

    • Refers to the atmospheric area above about 80 kilometers. Here, the volume percentage of different atmospheric components varies with altitude.

    ② The ionization characteristics of the atmosphere

    It is then divided into inosphere and neutral layer.

  • Ionosphere

    • Focusing on the ionosphere, it is mainly affected by the ionization of neutral atoms and air molecules by radiation outside the Earth (mainly solar radiation). The area above 60km above the surface is in a partially or fully ionized state. The ionosphere is a partially ionized atmospheric region; the fully ionized atmospheric region is called the magnetosphere. Of course, the magnetosphere can also be classified as part of the ionosphere.

    “Sir, by the way, in addition to the Earth, Venus, Mars, and Jupiter also have ionospheres!”

  • Neutral Layer

    • The neutral layer refers to the atmospheric layer from the ground to an altitude of about 60 kilometers. It is mainly composed of neutral gases. The atmosphere in the layer may sometimes have more charged particles in some parts (such as during thunderstorms), but generally, there are fewer charged particles.

    "Let's talk about your question."

  • Upper atmosphere

    • The upper atmosphere structure is composed of the ionosphere, an inhomogeneous layer, homogeneous layer, exosphere, thermosphere, and mesosphere. It refers to the area where The Earth’s atmosphere begins to ionize (i.e., above 60km above the surface). Similar to the ionosphere, people also have different views on the starting height of the upper atmosphere: Some believe that the lower limit of the upper atmosphere is about 30km above the surface, while others believe that the upper atmosphere is above the top of the mesosphere (85km above the surface). In addition, due to the solar radiation of the upper atmosphere, the atmospheric temperature above about 80 kilometers increases with the increase in altitude; at the same time, the particles in the upper atmosphere are very fast, and it is possible for them to overcome the gravitational pull of the Earth and escape from The Earth's atmosphere and gas particles in space may also enter the upper atmosphere. As a result, the air in the upper atmosphere is extremely thin, which can be said to be thinner than an artificial vacuum.

    “Sir, I have basically outlined the structure and characteristics of the atmosphere. I hope you can understand the principle!”

    “Great, I will continue to study and analyze it. Then let's move on to the second part! "I can't wait to say, I can't hold back my joy.”

    But this time, PETE is like a sophisticated old man, "Sir, I have to advise you, don't press yourself that hard! I understand your excitement and your love for this kind of thing, but everything needs to be done slowly, digested, and analyzed step by step to solve the problem more efficiently! Sir, I hope you can understand!"

    Because it transfers heat to the ground, it compensates for some of the heat lost by the ground to a certain extent, so it has a certain role in keeping the ground warm. By the way, when there are clouds in the sky (especially dense low clouds), atmospheric counter radiation is stronger, as it will absorb or reflect some radiation again.

    "Ah? It's not vertical movement, could it be horizontal movement?" I asked anxiously.

    "Sir, this requires you to understand the definition of atmospheric thermal circulation: thermal circulation is the simplest form of atmospheric movement, and air circulation is formed due to uneven heat and cold on the ground. The formation process is as follows: the atmosphere in the heated area expands and rises, forming low pressure near the ground and high pressure in the high altitude; the opposite is true in the cold area, thus forming a pressure difference between the horizontal plane near the ground and the high altitude; and it promotes the horizontal movement of the atmosphere, forming a thermal circulation at high and low altitudes. At the same altitude in the sky, the weight of the air column per unit area (i.e., air pressure) is large in the hot area, and the air pressure is small in the cold area, forming a hot-cold airflow at high altitude. After the airflow in the hot area is lost, the entire air column is reduced, low pressure is formed on the ground, and high pressure is formed in the cold area, forming a cold-hot airflow near the ground. Together with the rising and sinking airflows, a thermal circulation is formed."

    In fact, it is true that horizontal movement near the ground will form wind. In the meantime, air movement is generated under the action of forces. The forces acting on the air are gravity, pressure gradient force, Coriolis force, and ground friction. Near the ground, because the inertial centrifugal force is small and can be ignored, the wind is mainly affected by three forces: horizontal pressure gradient force, Coriolis force and friction. But before introducing the pressure gradient force, sir, I would like to ask you what is the pressure gradient. How is it generated?”

    "It seems that it is because the ground is heated unevenly, causing some air to rise and some air to sink, resulting in differences in air pressure on the same horizontal plane. The difference in air pressure per unit distance is the pressure gradient." I rejoindered.

    "Very good, then you should be able to understand what follows:

  • Pressure Gradient Force

    • It is a vector that is perpendicular to the isobaric surface and points from high pressure to low pressure. Its value is equal to the pressure difference within a unit distance. Although the horizontal pressure gradient is small, it is the starting force that drives the air movement and is the direct cause and power of the horizontal movement of the air.

  • Coriolis Force

    • While the air moves in the direction of the horizontal pressure gradient force on the rotating surface of the earth, it will be affected by the earth's rotational force. The Coriolis force values of different latitudes around the world are different. The Coriolis force on the equator is zero, the Coriolis force at the poles is the largest, and the Coriolis force at other latitudes is between the two. The direction of the Coriolis force points to the right of the object's movement in the northern hemisphere and to the left of the object's movement in the southern hemisphere. The Coriolis force is only generated when the air moves relative to the surface of the earth, and it only changes the direction of the air movement (wind direction) but not the speed of the air movement (wind speed).

  • Friction

    • Friction in atmospheric motion is generally divided into internal friction and external friction. Internal friction is a kind of restraining force generated between two air layers in contact with each other at different speeds or directions. Because of its small value, it is often not considered. External friction is the resistance of the underlying surface to the movement of air when the air moves close to the underlying surface(the interface between the atmosphere and the solid ground or liquid water surface below it). Its direction is opposite to the direction of air movement. At the same time, friction can reduce the speed of air movement and reduce the Coriolis force accordingly. In terms of the influence of friction, its influence on air is most significant near the ground, and gradually decreases with the increase of altitude. Above 1km-2km, the influence of friction is so small that it can be ignored. Therefore, the area below this altitude is called the friction layer, and the area above is called the free atmosphere.

    "Oh, so that's the case. Then I would like to ask, what are the friction layer and the free atmosphere, and what are their respective functions?"

  • Friction Layer

    • The friction layer is another name for the atmospheric boundary layer. It is at the bottom of the atmosphere. Because its thickness is related to many factors, it ranges from a few hundred meters to 1.5 to 2.0 kilometers, with an average of 1 kilometer. It is named because its movement state is obviously affected by the friction of the ground.

    The free atmosphere refers to the atmosphere far away from the ground and not affected by the friction of the ground. It usually refers to the atmosphere above 2000 meters. In the free atmosphere, due to the absence of friction, the movement of air is mainly affected by the pressure gradient force and the Coriolis force. In the free atmosphere, air movement basically obeys the geostrophic wind (where the horizontal movement of air in the free atmosphere when the horizontal pressure gradient force and the Coriolis force are balanced) or gradient wind law , and the airflow is almost parallel to the isobars.
